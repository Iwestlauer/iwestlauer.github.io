If you wear a smartwatch or use a health app, you're generating data that rivals what a hospital cardiac monitor produces — ECG readings, heart rate variability, blood oxygen levels, sleep staging, even stress scores. By some estimates, [close to half of US adults now use health apps](https://www.bcg.com/publications/2026/how-ai-agents-will-transform-health-care), and [440 million wearable devices shipped globally in 2024 alone](https://stealthcloud.ai/ai-privacy/ai-wearables-privacy/).

Here's the problem: almost none of that data is protected by HIPAA.

## The Gap

HIPAA only applies to "covered entities" — hospitals, insurers, and clearinghouses — and their business associates. Consumer tech companies like Fitbit (now owned by Google), Oura, and Whoop don't fall into those categories. That means your Apple Watch ECG and a hospital ECG can produce [clinically indistinguishable data](https://www.accountablehq.com/post/do-fitness-apps-need-to-be-hipaa-compliant-when-it-applies-and-when-it-doesn-t), but they receive fundamentally different legal treatment depending on who collected it.

This isn't just a technicality. AI models running on wearable data can now [infer depression risk, predict illness days before symptoms appear, and detect pregnancy](https://stealthcloud.ai/ai-privacy/ai-wearables-privacy/) from passive biometric signals. The sensitivity of the data has outpaced the legal framework designed to protect it.

## What the FTC Is Doing

In the absence of HIPAA coverage, the FTC has stepped in through its [Health Breach Notification Rule](https://www.ftc.gov/legal-library/browse/rules/health-breach-notification-rule) (HBNR). Originally adopted in 2009, the rule sat mostly dormant until 2021, when the FTC clarified that it applies to health apps and connected devices.

In 2024, [formal amendments expanded the rule's scope](https://www.ftc.gov/news-events/news/press-releases/2024/04/ftc-finalizes-changes-health-breach-notification-rule) to explicitly name fitness trackers and wearables. A key change: sharing health data with ad networks without user consent now qualifies as a breach. The FTC backed this up with enforcement — [GoodRx and the Premom fertility app](https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/resources/complying-ftcs-health-breach-notification-rule-0) both faced penalties in 2024–2025.

It's a meaningful step, but the HBNR only covers breaches. It doesn't establish broader privacy rights like access, deletion, or consent for data use.

## Enter HIPRA

That's where the [Health Information Privacy Reform Act](https://www.alstonprivacy.com/closing-the-privacy-gap-hipra-targets-health-apps-and-wearables/) (HIPRA) comes in. Introduced in November 2025 by Senator Bill Cassidy, HIPRA is essentially ["HIPAA for apps."](https://www.kmklaw.com/newsroom-publications-1812) It would extend three categories of requirements to consumer health technology:

- **Privacy** — consent before selling health data, minimum necessary standards, and rights to access, amend, and delete your information
- **Security** — NIST-aligned safeguards comparable to what hospitals follow
- **Breach notification** — standardized rules modeled on HIPAA, replacing today's patchwork

HIPRA introduces a new data category called ["Applicable Health Information"](https://privaplan.com/health-information-under-hipra-how-the-new-privacy-act-will-reshape-apps-and-consumer-data/) (AHI), which covers anything from heart rate data to AI-inferred health indicators. It's still a bill, not law — but the direction is clear.

## Meanwhile, AI Isn't Waiting

While regulation catches up, healthcare AI is [shifting from experimentation to execution in 2026](https://enterprises.upmc.com/resources/insights/health-care-innovation-in-2026-from-ai-buzz-to-measurable-impact/). Ambient AI scribes are reducing physician documentation burden. [Context-aware clinical decision support](https://www.merative.com/blog/real-world-data-trends-2026-the-shift-to-quality-and-ai-precision) is replacing static drug-interaction alerts. Wearable data is being integrated directly into EHR workflows for chronic disease management.

All of this depends on data — and increasingly, on data that flows from consumer devices into clinical systems. As [BCG notes](https://www.bcg.com/publications/2026/how-ai-agents-will-transform-health-care), providers will use AI to analyze personal device data alongside genetic information and medical records to predict health problems before they start.

The promise is real. But so is the risk: the more health data AI consumes, the higher the stakes around who controls and protects it.

## What You Can Do Now

Whether you're building health tech, studying it, or just wearing a fitness tracker, here are a few practical steps:

1. **Audit your own data.** Review the privacy settings and data-sharing permissions on your health apps and wearables. Disable features you don't actively use.
2. **Read the privacy policy.** Specifically look for language about third-party sharing, ad targeting, and research partnerships.
3. **Follow the regulatory trajectory.** HIPRA, the updated FTC rule, and state-level consumer health data laws (like Washington's My Health My Data Act) are shaping what compliance will look like in the next few years.
4. **Think about data governance early.** If you're working on health data projects, build privacy-by-design principles into your workflow now — don't wait for regulation to force it.

The gap between how sensitive our health data is and how well the law protects it is closing. The question is whether it closes fast enough.
