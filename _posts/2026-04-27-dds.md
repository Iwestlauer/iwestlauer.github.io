# From Overwhelmed to Growing Roots: My First Semester in Doing Data Science

I went into my first semester of a Master’s in Data Science knowing I’d be challenged—but I didn’t expect to enjoy the chaos as much as I did. *Doing Data Science* quickly became one of those classes that throws you straight into the deep end: massive datasets, unfamiliar concepts, and just enough direction to figure things out on your own. There were definitely moments of panic—like when my first project was late and I was convinced I had completely broken everything, only to discover the fix was… restarting my R session. But somewhere between debugging code, wrestling with messy data, and slowly making sense of concepts like hypothesis testing, I realized I wasn’t just surviving the course—I was actually starting to think like a data scientist. And that shift made all the difference.

## What “Doing Data Science” Really Means

Going into the class, I thought “doing data science” would mostly mean cleaning data, running analyses, and maybe using tools like Python or SQL to get answers. Instead, the course completely reshaped that definition. There was no Python or SQL—we worked in R, which ended up being a really fun shift, and having a Python background actually gave me a helpful head start in thinking through problems. What surprised me most, though, was how much broader the experience was. We touched on machine learning, built interactive R Shiny apps, and spent a lot of time on something I hadn’t fully expected: professional communication. It wasn’t just about getting the right answer—it was about explaining it clearly, presenting it effectively, and making sure the story behind the data actually made sense to other people.

## The Challenges (and the Branches I Had to Climb)

The challenges in this course were real, and not always in the ways I expected. Some of them were technical—like trying to work with massive datasets without bringing R to a halt, running into cryptic package issues like Arrow errors, or accidentally creating multiple versions of the same variable after a messy join and having to untangle what went wrong. Other times it was more conceptual, like trying to get a real intuition for p-values, hypothesis testing, and what uncertainty actually looks like in practice. And then there were the moments where everything just felt tangled—code wouldn’t run, outputs didn’t make sense, and I had to retrace my steps more than once to figure out where things went off. But in a way, those were the moments where I did the most branching out: every time something broke, I had to explore a new path to fix it, and that’s usually where the real understanding started to grow.

## Breakthrough Moments

One of the biggest breakthrough moments for me came during my first project on attrition, when I finally started to really understand sensitivity versus specificity—not just as definitions, but as trade-offs that actually matter. Before this project, I would’ve said I understood them, but I mostly just knew which formula went with which term and how to calculate them from a classification matrix. Working through the problem in context changed that completely. I could see how sensitivity captures how well a model identifies true positives, while specificity focuses on correctly identifying true negatives, and more importantly, how improving one often comes at the cost of the other. Once I saw that tension play out in my own analysis, it clicked in a much deeper way. It wasn’t just about calculating metrics anymore—it was about thinking carefully about what kind of mistakes were more acceptable, or more expensive, in a real-world setting.

## The Coolest Things I Learned

Some of the coolest things I learned this semester were the skills that made me feel like I was actually starting to *do* data science, not just study it. I got comfortable wrangling messy, real-world data—cleaning it, transforming it, and creating features that actually mattered for analysis. I built visualizations that weren’t just decorative, but told a clear story about what was happening in the data, and I started to think more intentionally about how to communicate insights rather than just compute them. On the statistical side, concepts like hypothesis testing, permutations, and uncertainty went from abstract ideas to tools I could actively use to interpret results. I also learned how to work with time series data and recognize when patterns *weren’t* there, which is just as important as finding them. Looking back, I’m genuinely proud of how much I’ve picked up in a short amount of time—not because it was easy, but because I stuck with it long enough for things to start making sense.

## What I’d Tell Myself (and Where I Am Now)

One of the biggest things I’ve learned this semester is that you don’t need to know everything at the start—you just need to know how to start. When I got stuck, my first move became doing a bit of research on my own, whether that was digging through documentation, finding explanations online, or using resources like Kaggle to see how other people approached similar problems. From there, it became much easier to ask better questions when I did reach out for help. I learned to lean on peers first, and then take those questions into office hours with my professor, which turned out to be one of the most valuable parts of the course. Even when I didn’t have something specific to ask, just having that time to talk through what I was learning helped me process everything more clearly.

What changed most over the semester wasn’t just my technical ability—it was my confidence. Having a cohort and professor who were going through the same challenges made a huge difference. There was a shared sense of figuring it out together, even when things were messy or uncertain. So a big shoutout to Diane, Lindsay, Josh, Aaron, Andy, Zach, Avery, Chase, Joshua, and Roger—we made it through the first semester, and the immersion conference was honestly a blast. I’m finishing this course not just with new skills, but with a real sense that I belong in this space, even when I’m still learning how to branch out.
